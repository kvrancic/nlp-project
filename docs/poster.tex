  \begin{block}{1. The Question}

    \heading{Zhao et al.\ (NeurIPS 2025)}
    Multilingual LLMs reason worse in non-English languages.
    Zhao et al.\ showed this is partly caused by \emph{language-specific
    representations interfering with reasoning}.
    Their method: compute per-language mean hidden states across translated
    math problems, extract a ``language subspace'' via SVD, and project it
    out at inference time ($\hat{h} = h - \lambda\, M_s M_s^\top h$).
    Across 10 models and 10 languages: consistent gains up to +12.8pp,
    especially for low-resource languages.

    \heading{Our extension}
    Zhao's method operates on entire subspaces and cannot identify individual
    features or explain the mechanism.
    We decompose the effect to individual SAE features to characterize both
    the \textbf{mechanism} and the \textbf{nature} of the ablated
    representations.

    \heading{The question}
    \textbf{What are these features doing, and what are they encoding?}

    Using SAEs on Gemma 3 4B IT, we identify candidate
    features via monolinguality ($\nu$) $\cap$ supervised probe.
    Ablation improves reasoning for en~+13.2pp, es~+6.8pp --- but three
    lines of evidence suggest the label ``language feature'' is a
    \textbf{procedural tag}, not an \textbf{intrinsic property}.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{2. SAE Language Feature Extraction}

    We pass 250 MGSM math problems $\times$ 5 languages through Gemma 3 4B IT,
    extract last-token residual-stream activations, and encode them through
    Gemma Scope 2 SAEs (16k-width, layers \{9, 17, 22, 29\}).

    \heading{Two identification methods}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Method A --- Monolinguality ($\nu$):}
            $\nu_s^L = \mu_s^L - \gamma_s^L$ (mean activation on target language
            minus mean across other languages). Top-50 per language.
      \item \textbf{Method B --- Supervised probe:}
            Logistic regression on SAE feature vectors (5-way language
            classification, accuracy ceiling $\sim$0.88). Top-50 per language
            by coefficient magnitude.
    \end{itemize}
    Candidates $=$ A $\cap$ B intersection.
    Methods mostly disagree (Jaccard 0.03--0.33) --- the intersection selects
    features that \emph{both} methods agree are language-differential.

    \heading{Identifying reasoning features}
    MGSM contains the same 250 math problems translated into each language.
    A feature is tagged as \textbf{reasoning} if it activates (above
    threshold) in \emph{all 5 languages for the same problem} on $\geq$10\%
    of problems --- i.e., it responds to the mathematical content, not the
    language. This reduces 16{,}384 SAE features to just 19 at layer~17
    (0.12\%).

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{3. Ablation Results}

    \heading{A. Downstream accuracy}
    Ablate top-$k$ candidate features per language at layer 17 using
    directional (QR-orthogonalized) projection.
    $k{=}20$ selected on 50-prompt dev split.

    \begin{center}
    {\small\textbf{MGSM accuracy after language-feature ablation (layer 17, $k{=}20$)}}
    \vspace{0.3em}

    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
    \begin{tabular}{l c c c}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{Baseline} & \textbf{Zhao SVD} & \textbf{SAE ablation} \\
    \midrule
    en & 0.580 & 0.632 & \textbf{0.712} \\
    zh & 0.624 & 0.624 & 0.596 \\
    es & 0.568 & 0.600 & \textbf{0.636} \\
    bn & 0.564 & 0.564 & 0.480 \\
    sw & 0.320 & 0.332 & 0.296 \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Mean} & 0.531 & 0.550 & 0.544 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    Mean accuracy under SAE ablation (0.544) is comparable to Zhao's
    subspace baseline (0.550), confirming that individual-feature ablation
    replicates the subspace-level effect.
    SAE ablation outperforms Zhao for English (+8.0pp) and Spanish (+3.6pp),
    suggesting feature-level targeting can be more precise than subspace
    projection for some languages.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{}

    \heading{B. Which features affect performance?}
    Not all statistically-identified candidates behave alike.
    We ablate each of the top-5 A$\cap$B features per language individually
    and measure (i)~perplexity delta on target-language text and
    (ii)~accuracy delta on MGSM reasoning.
    A feature is tagged \textbf{LANGUAGE} if ablation raises perplexity
    but does not hurt accuracy (language-only);
    \textbf{SHARED} if it raises perplexity \emph{and} drops accuracy
    (language and reasoning entangled).

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.1}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l r r r}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{LANG} & \textbf{SHARED} & \textbf{Other} \\
    \midrule
    en & \textbf{5} & 0 & 0 \\
    es & \textbf{3} & 0 & 2 \\
    zh & 3 & 2 & 0 \\
    sw & 2 & \textbf{3} & 0 \\
    bn & 0 & \textbf{5} & 0 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    English's top-5 are all cleanly LANGUAGE; Bengali's are all SHARED.
    This directly predicts the asymmetry in 3A: ablating clean language
    features helps; ablating shared features hurts.

    \heading{C. What happens to the reasoning features?}
    We ablate the same language features at L17 and measure how the 21
    reasoning features respond (forward-only, 50 problems $\times$ 5 langs,
    Wilcoxon signed-rank).
    Neuronpedia look-ups reveal three functional camps:

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.1}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l l l}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Camp} & \textbf{Neuronpedia identity} & \textbf{Effect} \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Reasoning} & solving for $x$, step-by-step, & \textbf{Stable} \\
    {\scriptsize (4094, 5993, 1105)} & causal reasoning & $|\Delta|{<}200$ \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Answering} & explanatory, QA patterns, & \textbf{Boosted} \\
    {\scriptsize (96, 406, 377, 712, 9240)} & response initiation & $\Delta$ up to +7036 \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Answering} & conversational acknowledgment, & \textbf{Suppressed} \\
    {\scriptsize (107, 279, \ldots)} & preamble (``Okay,\ldots'') & to $\approx$0 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.85\linewidth]{figures/neuronpedia_feat1105.png}
      \vspace*{-0.5em}
      \caption{Feature 1105 (L17) on Neuronpedia: fires on
        causal/sequential reasoning (``let's break this down,''
        ``step-by-step''). This genuine reasoning feature is
        \textbf{unaffected} by language-feature ablation ($\Delta{<}200$).}
    \end{figure}

    \textbf{Ablation reorganizes answering, not reasoning.}
    The genuine reasoning features (4094, 5993, 1105) are
    \textbf{already active and independent of language}.
    What changes are the \emph{answering} features: explanatory
    features get boosted while conversational preamble gets suppressed
    --- the model shifts toward more direct answer generation.
    This explains the en/es asymmetry: clean LANGUAGE features ablate
    without side effects, reshuffling answer generation; SHARED features
    (bn/sw) disrupt more than just language, so the improvement doesn't
    come through.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{4. But What Does It Mean to Be a Language Feature?}

    \heading{A. Humanly-interpretable features --- through Neuronpedia}
    We look up every chosen feature on Neuronpedia.
    Even for \textbf{English and Spanish} --- our best-performing
    languages --- only 2--3 of the top features are humanly interpretable
    as language-specific.
    For \textbf{Bengali and Swahili}, \emph{none} map to recognizable
    language markers.

    We hand-selected only Neuronpedia-confirmed features and ablated them
    (5--6 per language, $n{=}50$).
    \textbf{Result: no detectable change in reasoning performance}
    ($\pm$0--4pp, indistinguishable from random ablation).
    The causally effective features are the statistically-identified ones
    that lack clear interpretability.

    \heading{B. Are these language features global?}
    Both Zhao's subspace and our SAE features are identified on math
    content. We run the \emph{same} identification procedure on
    FLORES-200 (1012 parallel translations, no math) and compare.

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.60\linewidth]{figures/jaccard_overlap.pdf}
      \vspace*{-0.5em}
      \caption{Same procedure, different domain $\to$ different features.
        English (J$=$0.15) and Spanish (J$=$0.19) share almost none.}
    \end{figure}

    The features identified as ``language features'' on MGSM are largely
    \textbf{different} from those identified on FLORES.
    A language probe trained on MGSM features transfers poorly to FLORES
    (accuracy 0.714, need ${>}$0.80), and the LANGUAGE/SHARED distinction
    from Section~3B is meaningless out of domain ($p{=}0.51$).
    If these were genuinely encoding \emph{language identity}, they would
    generalize across domains. They don't --- they encode
    \textbf{task-specific structure that co-varies with language}
    on a particular dataset.

  \end{block}

  \begin{exampleblock}{5. Conclusion}

    \begin{enumerate}
      \item \textbf{``Language feature'' is a method-dependent category.}
            Every procedure for identifying language features depends on
            what definition you start from, and no definition is privileged.

      \item \textbf{The procedure works.}
            Statistical identification + ablation reproduces and extends
            Zhao's gains (en~+13.2pp, es~+6.8pp).

      \item \textbf{But these are not reliably language features.}
            They don't generalize out of domain (probe transfer~0.714,
            separation $p{=}0.51$), don't match Neuronpedia auto-interp,
            and change when the domain changes (en Jaccard$=$0.15).

      \item \textbf{``Reasoning feature'' is also a procedural tag.}
            The most dramatic released feature (96, $\Delta$ up to +7036)
            fires on word-problem content tokens, not mathematical
            operations. Both labels are method-dependent.
    \end{enumerate}

    \heading{Limitations \& future work}
    {\small Single model (Gemma 3 4B IT), single task (MGSM), 5 languages;
    global $k{=}20$ suboptimal; dev/test overlap in profiling step.
    Next step: compare across feature identification operationalizations
    (causal LM ablation, top-activating examples, activation-steering).}
