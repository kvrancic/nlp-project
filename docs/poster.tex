  \begin{block}{1. The Question}

    \heading{Zhao et al.\ (NeurIPS 2025)}
    Multilingual LLMs reason worse in non-English languages.
    Zhao et al.\ showed this is partly caused by \emph{language-specific
    representations interfering with reasoning}.
    Their method: compute per-language mean hidden states across translated
    math problems, extract a ``language subspace'' via SVD, and project it
    out at inference time ($\hat{h} = h - \lambda\, M_s M_s^\top h$).
    Across 10 models and 10 languages: consistent gains up to +12.8pp,
    especially for low-resource languages.

    \heading{Our extension}
    Zhao's method operates on entire subspaces and cannot identify individual
    features or explain the mechanism.
    We decompose the effect to individual SAE features to characterize both
    the \textbf{mechanism} and the \textbf{nature} of the ablated
    representations.

    \heading{The question}
    \textbf{What are these features doing, and what are they encoding?}

    Using SAEs on Gemma 3 4B IT, we identify candidate
    features via monolinguality ($\nu$) $\cap$ supervised probe.
    Ablation improves reasoning for en~+13.2pp, es~+6.8pp --- but three
    lines of evidence suggest the label ``language feature'' is a
    \textbf{procedural tag}, not an \textbf{intrinsic property}.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{2. SAE Language Feature Extraction}

    We pass 250 MGSM math problems $\times$ 5 languages through Gemma 3 4B IT,
    extract last-token residual-stream activations, and encode them through
    Gemma Scope 2 SAEs (16k-width, layers \{9, 17, 22, 29\}).

    \heading{Two identification methods}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Method A --- Monolinguality ($\nu$):}
            $\nu_s^L = \mu_s^L - \gamma_s^L$ (mean activation on target language
            minus mean across other languages). Top-50 per language.
      \item \textbf{Method B --- Supervised probe:}
            Logistic regression on SAE feature vectors (5-way language
            classification, accuracy ceiling $\sim$0.88). Top-50 per language
            by coefficient magnitude.
    \end{itemize}
    Candidates $=$ A $\cap$ B intersection.
    Methods mostly disagree (Jaccard 0.03--0.33) --- the intersection selects
    features that \emph{both} methods agree are language-differential.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{3. The Procedure Works}

    Ablate top-$k$ candidate features per language at layer 17 using
    directional (QR-orthogonalized) projection.
    $k{=}20$ selected on 50-prompt dev split.

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.15}
    \begin{tabular}{l c c c}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{Baseline} & \textbf{Zhao SVD} & \textbf{SAE ablation} \\
    \midrule
    en & 0.580 & 0.632 & \textbf{0.712} \\
    zh & 0.624 & 0.624 & 0.596 \\
    es & 0.568 & 0.600 & \textbf{0.636} \\
    bn & 0.564 & 0.564 & 0.480 \\
    sw & 0.320 & 0.332 & 0.296 \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Mean} & 0.531 & 0.550 & 0.544 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    Mean accuracy under SAE ablation (0.544) is comparable to Zhao's
    subspace baseline (0.550), confirming that individual-feature ablation
    replicates the subspace-level effect.
    SAE ablation outperforms Zhao for English (+8.0pp) and Spanish (+3.6pp),
    suggesting feature-level targeting can be more precise than subspace
    projection for some languages.

  \end{block}

  \begin{alertblock}{4. But What Does It Mean to Be a Language Feature?}

    Zhao identifies the subspace on translated math problems; we identify
    our SAE features on MGSM. Both identifications happen on math content.
    Whether the resulting components generalize as language features on
    neutral text has not been previously tested.

    \heading{A. Causal profiling of individual features}
    Not all statistically-identified candidates behave alike.
    We ablate each of the top-5 A$\cap$B features per language individually
    and measure (i)~perplexity delta on target-language text and
    (ii)~accuracy delta on MGSM reasoning.
    A feature is tagged \textbf{LANGUAGE} if ablation raises perplexity
    but does not hurt accuracy (language-only);
    \textbf{SHARED} if it raises perplexity \emph{and} drops accuracy
    (language and reasoning entangled).

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.1}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l r r r r}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{LANG} & \textbf{SHARED} & \textbf{REAS.} & \textbf{JUNK} \\
    \midrule
    en & \textbf{5} & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
    es & \textbf{3} & 0 & 1 & 1 \\
    zh & 3 & 2 & 0 & 0 \\
    sw & 2 & \textbf{3} & 0 & 0 \\
    bn & 0 & \textbf{5} & 0 & 0 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    English's top-5 are all cleanly LANGUAGE; Bengali's are all SHARED.
    This directly predicts the asymmetry in Panel~3: ablating clean language
    features helps; ablating shared features hurts.

    \heading{B. Humanly-interpretable features --- through Neuronpedia}
    We look up LANGUAGE-tagged features on Neuronpedia to check whether
    they correspond to recognizable language markers.
    For \textbf{English and Spanish}, top features map to recognizable
    language markers --- and ablation \emph{outperforms} Zhao
    (en~+8.0pp, es~+3.6pp over SVD).
    For \textbf{Bengali and Swahili}, none of the top features map to
    recognizable language markers --- and ablation \emph{underperforms}
    Zhao (bn~$-$8.4pp, sw~$-$3.6pp vs baseline).

    Running the \emph{same} procedure on FLORES vs MGSM yields largely
    \emph{different} feature sets (layer~17).

    \textbf{The distinction:} ``language feature'' as used in our pipeline is a
    \textbf{procedural tag} --- derived from cross-language statistical
    comparison on a specific dataset --- not an \textbf{intrinsic property}
    of the feature.

  \begin{block}{5. Mechanism: Capacity Competition}

    Regardless of \emph{what} the ablated features encode, the mechanism is
    capacity competition: language and reasoning features compete for
    residual-stream bandwidth.

    Ablate top-20 features at L17, measure activation change of 21 candidate
    reasoning features (50 problems $\times$ 5 langs, Wilcoxon signed-rank).

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{figures/feat96_dormant.pdf}
      \vspace*{-0.5em}
      \caption{Feature 96 is completely dormant at baseline but activates
        strongly (2345--7036) across all languages after ablation.
        All $p < 1.8 \times 10^{-15}$.}
    \end{figure}

    \textbf{Dormant $\to$ active:} Feature 96 is zero normally but explodes
    to 2345--7036 when features are removed --- released reasoning capacity.
    \textbf{Active $\to$ suppressed:} Feature 34 collapses from 2177 to 0 ---
    representational space reclaimed.
    This explains why ablation works \emph{regardless of what the ablated
    features ``are''}: it is about representational pressure, not about
    removing ``language.''

  \end{block}

  \begin{exampleblock}{6. Takeaways}

    \begin{enumerate}
      \item \textbf{The procedure works.}
            Statistical ``language feature'' identification + ablation
            reproduces and extends Zhao's gains (en~+13.2pp, es~+6.8pp).

      \item \textbf{But these are not reliably language features.}
            They don't generalize out of domain (probe transfer~0.714,
            separation $p{=}0.51$), don't match Neuronpedia auto-interp,
            and change when the domain changes (en Jaccard$=$0.15).

      \item \textbf{The label is procedural.}
            ``Language feature'' captures task-specific co-occurrence with
            language identity, not an intrinsic property.

      \item \textbf{Capacity competition is the mechanism.}
            Ablation releases dormant reasoning features ($\Delta$ up to
            +7036, $p < 10^{-15}$) no matter what the ablated features ``are.''
    \end{enumerate}

    \heading{Limitations}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item Single model (Gemma 3 4B IT), single task (MGSM), 5 languages
      \item Global $k{=}20$ suboptimal; per-language $k$ would improve zh/sw/bn
      \item Dev/test overlap in profiling step
    \end{itemize}
