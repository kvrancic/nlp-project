  \begin{block}{1. The Question}

    Counter-intuitively, removing language-specific information from a
    model's hidden state can \emph{improve} multilingual reasoning. We use
    sparse autoencoders (SAEs)~[1], which decompose hidden
    states into interpretable individual features, to ask what those removed
    representations actually are.

    \heading{Zhao et al.\ (NeurIPS 2025)}
    Zhao et al.~[2] showed that multilingual reasoning is partly degraded
    by \emph{language-specific representations interfering with reasoning}.
    Their method projects out a per-language mean subspace via top-$r$ SVD
    at inference: $\hat{h} = h - \lambda M_s M_s^\top h$ (we use $r{=}3$ in
    our reproduction). Across 10 models and 11 languages: consistent gains
    up to +14.0pp, especially for low-resource languages.

    \heading{Our extension}
    Zhao's method operates on entire subspaces and cannot identify individual
    features or explain the mechanism. We use SAEs to decompose the effect
    into individual interpretable features and characterize both the
    \textbf{mechanism} and the \textbf{nature} of the ablated
    representations.

    \heading{The question}
    \textbf{What are these features doing, and what are they encoding?}

    Using SAEs on Gemma 3 4B IT, we identify candidate features via
    monolinguality ($\nu$) intersected with a supervised language probe.
    The ``language'' label is induced by this selection criterion; no
    human inspects a feature and assigns it.
    Ablation improves reasoning for en~(+13.2pp) and es~(+6.8pp). Three
    lines of evidence characterize this induced label as a
    \textbf{procedural tag} whose scope is determined by the selection
    method that produced it.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{2. SAE Language Feature Extraction}

    We pass 250 MGSM math problems~[3] $\times$ 5 languages through
    Gemma 3 4B IT, extract last-token residual-stream activations, and encode
    them through Gemma Scope 2 SAEs~[4] (16k-width,
    layers \{9, 17, 22, 29\}).

    \heading{Two identification methods}
    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Method A. Monolinguality ($\nu$)}~[5]:
            $\nu_s^L = \mu_s^L - \gamma_s^L$ (mean activation on target language
            minus mean across other languages). Top-50 per language.
      \item \textbf{Method B. Supervised probe:}
            Logistic regression on SAE feature vectors (5-way language
            classification, accuracy ceiling $\sim$0.88). Top-50 per language
            by coefficient magnitude.
    \end{itemize}
    Candidates $=$ A $\cap$ B intersection.
    Methods mostly disagree (Jaccard 0.03--0.33); the intersection selects
    features that \emph{both} methods agree are language-differential.

    \heading{Identifying reasoning features}
    MGSM contains the same 250 math problems translated into each language.
    A feature is tagged as \textbf{reasoning} if it activates (above
    threshold) in \emph{all 5 languages for the same problem} on $\geq$10\%
    of problems. This criterion selects features whose activation tracks
    mathematical content across language translations.
    Of 16{,}384 SAE features at layer~17, 19 satisfy it (0.12\%).

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{3. Ablation Results}

    \heading{A. Downstream accuracy}
    Ablate top-$k$ candidate features per language at layer 17 using
    directional (QR-orthogonalized) projection. Prior work shows
    single-feature SAE steering already controls model output language
    [6]; we extend this to multi-feature ablation and ask whether
    \emph{reasoning} also responds. $k{=}20$ selected on 50-prompt dev
    split.

    \begin{center}
    {\small\textbf{MGSM accuracy after language-feature ablation (layer 17, $k{=}20$)}}
    \vspace{0.3em}

    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.05}
    \begin{tabular}{l c c c r r}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{Baseline} & \textbf{Zhao SVD}
      & \textbf{SAE} & \textbf{$\Delta$ vs base} & \textbf{$\Delta$ vs Zhao} \\
    \midrule
    en   & 0.580 & 0.632 & \textbf{0.712} & \textbf{+13.2} & \textbf{+8.0} \\
    es   & 0.568 & 0.600 & \textbf{0.636} & \textbf{+6.8}  & \textbf{+3.6} \\
    zh   & 0.624 & 0.624 & 0.596          & $-$2.8         & $-$2.8 \\
    sw   & 0.320 & 0.332 & 0.296          & $-$2.4         & $-$3.6 \\
    bn   & 0.564 & 0.564 & 0.480          & $-$8.4         & $-$8.4 \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Mean} & 0.531 & 0.550 & 0.544 & +1.3 & $-$0.6 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \\[0.3em]
    {\footnotesize $\Delta$ values in absolute percentage points.}
    \end{center}

    Mean accuracy (0.544) matches Zhao's subspace baseline (0.550),
    confirming feature-level ablation replicates the subspace effect.
    SAE outperforms Zhao on en (+8.0pp) and es (+3.6pp): per-feature
    targeting is more precise than subspace projection for some languages.

    \textbf{But the asymmetry is striking}: en~+13.2pp and es~+6.8pp
    \emph{gain}, while bn~$-$8.4pp and sw~$-$2.4pp \emph{lose}. Why?
    Section~3B opens up the top-5 features per language and shows that
    different languages have qualitatively different feature taxonomies.

    \heading{Layer-wise (H3)}
    Sweeping the procedure across L9/17/22/29 (top-10 A$\cap$B,
    unfiltered) yields an inverted-U peaking at L22 (+6.1pp avg);
    L22 is the only layer at which all 5 languages gain. Unfiltered
    L17 underperforms, which validates the causal LANGUAGE/SHARED
    filter used in the table above.

  \end{block}

    \heading{B. Which features affect performance?}
    Not all statistically-identified candidates behave alike.
    We ablate each of the top-5 A$\cap$B features per language individually
    and measure (i)~perplexity delta on target-language text and
    (ii)~accuracy delta on MGSM reasoning.
    A feature is tagged \textbf{LANGUAGE} if ablation raises perplexity
    but does not hurt accuracy (language-only);
    \textbf{SHARED} if it raises perplexity \emph{and} drops accuracy
    (language and reasoning entangled).

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l r r r}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Lang} & \textbf{LANG} & \textbf{SHARED} & \textbf{Other} \\
    \midrule
    en & \textbf{5} & 0 & 0 \\
    es & \textbf{3} & 0 & 2 \\
    zh & 3 & 2 & 0 \\
    sw & 2 & \textbf{3} & 0 \\
    bn & 0 & \textbf{5} & 0 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \textbf{English's top-5 are all cleanly LANGUAGE; Bengali's are all
    SHARED.} Languages whose top features cleanly separate from reasoning
    gain accuracy when ablated (en, es); those whose top features entangle
    with reasoning lose accuracy (bn, sw). The 3A asymmetry and the 3B
    taxonomy are \textbf{two views of the same phenomenon}; \S3C now
    makes the mechanism explicit.

    \heading{C. What happens to the reasoning features?}
    We ablate the same language features at L17 and measure how the 21
    reasoning features respond (forward-only, 50 problems $\times$ 5 langs,
    Wilcoxon signed-rank), in the spirit of feature-circuit attribution~[7].
    Neuronpedia look-ups reveal three functional camps:

    \begin{center}
    \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.0}
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{l l l}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Camp} & \textbf{Neuronpedia identity} & \textbf{Effect} \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Reasoning} & solving for $x$, step-by-step, & \textbf{Stable} \\
    {\scriptsize (4094, 5993, 1105)} & causal reasoning & $|\Delta|{<}200$ \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Answering} & explanatory, QA patterns, & \textbf{Boosted} \\
    {\scriptsize (96, 406, 377, 712, 9240)} & response initiation & $\Delta$ up to +7036 \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Answering} & conversational acknowledgment, & \textbf{Suppressed} \\
    {\scriptsize (107, 279, \ldots)} & preamble (``Okay,\ldots'') & to $\approx$0 \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{center}

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.82\linewidth]{figures/neuronpedia_feat1105.png}
      \vspace*{-0.8em}
      \caption{Feature 1105 (L17): fires on causal/sequential reasoning
        (``let's break this down''). \textbf{Unaffected} by language-feature
        ablation ($\Delta{<}200$).}
    \end{figure}

    \textbf{Ablation reorganizes the answering pathway while reasoning
    features remain stable} ($|\Delta|{<}200$ for 4094, 5993, 1105 across
    all 5 langs). Explanatory features get boosted while conversational
    preamble is suppressed, shifting the model toward direct answer
    generation. This explains the en/es asymmetry: clean LANGUAGE features
    ablate without side effects; SHARED features (bn/sw) disrupt reasoning
    along with language, blocking the gain.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{4. But What Does It Mean to Be a Language Feature?}

    Two definitions of ``language feature'' are in scope.
    \textbf{(i)~Statistical:} activation differs across languages
    (our selection procedure).
    \textbf{(ii)~Semantic:} top activations are humanly interpretable
    as encoding a specific language.
    We evaluate both via human recognizability (\S4A, col.~3) and
    out-of-domain stability (\S4B, col.~3); the two definitions return
    different feature sets.

  \end{block}

  \begin{block}{4. (cont.)}

    \heading{A. Humanly-interpretable features (via Neuronpedia)}
    We look up every chosen feature on Neuronpedia, an automated
    interpretability database that labels SAE features by top-K activating
    examples (the standard auto-interp protocol from~[1]). This protocol
    characterizes peak activations and may miss features whose mass sits
    in the moderate-magnitude tail; the Sonnet study~[8] flags this as
    an open limitation. We treat Neuronpedia labels as hypotheses for
    causal validation.
    Even for \textbf{English and Spanish}, our best-performing languages,
    only 2--3 of the top features are humanly interpretable as
    language-specific. For \textbf{Bengali and Swahili}, \emph{none} map
    to recognizable language markers.

    We hand-selected only Neuronpedia-confirmed features and ablated them
    (5--6 per language, $n{=}50$, both clamped and directional methods).
    \textbf{Result: no detectable change} ($\pm$0--4pp, indistinguishable
    from random ablation). The causally effective features are the
    statistically-identified ones lacking clear interpretability;
    Neuronpedia-validated picks alone produce null, confirming that the
    causal LANGUAGE/SHARED taxonomy carries the intervention effect.

    \heading{B. Are these language features global?}
    Both Zhao's subspace and our SAE features are identified on math
    content. We run the \emph{same} identification procedure on
    FLORES-200 (1012 parallel translations, no math) and compare.

    \begin{figure}
      \centering
      \includegraphics[width=0.50\linewidth]{figures/jaccard_overlap.pdf}
      \vspace*{-0.8em}
      \caption{Same procedure, different domain $\to$ different features.
        English (J$=$0.15) and Spanish (J$=$0.19) share almost none.}
    \end{figure}

    The features identified as ``language features'' on MGSM are largely
    \textbf{different} from those identified on FLORES.
    A language probe trained on MGSM features transfers poorly to FLORES
    (accuracy 0.714, need ${>}$0.80), and the LANGUAGE/SHARED distinction
    from Section~3B is meaningless out of domain ($p{=}0.51$).
    If these were genuinely encoding \emph{language identity} in the
    domain-general sense documented for multilingual LLMs~[9],
    they would generalize across domains. The Jaccard overlap and
    probe-transfer results indicate that they encode
    \textbf{task-specific structure that co-varies with language} on a
    particular dataset.

  \end{block}

  \begin{exampleblock}{5. Conclusion}

    \begin{enumerate}
      \item \textbf{``Language feature'' is an implicit label.}
            It is induced by the selection procedure (monolinguality
            $\cap$~probe); a different procedure (top-K activations on
            Neuronpedia) returns a different feature set. No
            operationalization is privileged.

      \item \textbf{The procedure works.}
            Statistical identification + ablation reproduces and extends
            Zhao's gains (en~+13.2pp, es~+6.8pp).

      \item \textbf{The features behave as MGSM-specific structure.}
            They fail to generalize out of domain (probe transfer~0.714,
            separation $p{=}0.51$), disagree with Neuronpedia auto-interp,
            and change identity across domains (en Jaccard$=$0.15). Their
            scope is bounded to the dataset on which they were identified.

      \item \textbf{``Reasoning feature'' is also a procedural tag.}
            The most dramatic released feature (96, $\Delta$ up to +7036)
            responds to narrative phrasing in word problems and activates
            only weakly on arithmetic operators, placing it in the
            answering camp. Both labels are method-dependent.
    \end{enumerate}

    \heading{Discussion: when a feature fires vs.\ what it does}
    {\small Correlational measures (top-activating examples, monolinguality,
    probe coefficients, auto-interp) characterize \emph{when} a feature
    fires; characterizing \emph{what} it does requires causal intervention.
    The label depends jointly on the selection procedure and the evaluative
    measure; we treat such labels as hypotheses for causal validation.}

    \heading{Limitations \& future work}
    {\small Single model (Gemma 3 4B IT), single task (MGSM), 5 languages;
    global $k{=}20$ suboptimal; dev/test overlap in profiling step.
    Next: compare across operationalizations (causal LM ablation,
    top-activating examples, activation steering); confirmed-only ablation
    using only causally-validated features per language.}
